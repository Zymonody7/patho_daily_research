Abstract. Large language models (LLMs) have shown promise in healthcare applications, however, their use in clinical practice is still limited by diagnostic hallucinations and insufficiently interpretable reasoning. We present MedCol-lab, a novel multi-agent framework that emulates the hierarchical consultation workflow of modern hospitals to autonomously navigate the full-cycle diagnostic process. The framework incorporates a dynamic specialist recruitment mech-anism that adaptively assembles clinical and examination agents according to patient-specific symptoms and examination results. To ensure the rigor of clinical work, we adopt a structured Issue-Based Information System (IBIS) argumen-tation protocol that requires agents to provide “Positions” backed by traceable evidence from medical knowledge and clinical data. Furthermore, the framework constructs a Hierarchical Disease Causal Chain that transforms flattened diag-nostic predictions into a structured model of pathological progression through explicit logical operators. A multi-round Consensus Mechanism iteratively fil-ters low-quality reasoning through logic auditing and weighted voting. Evaluated on real-world clinical datasets, MedCollab significantly outperforms pure LLMs and medical multi-agent systems in Accuracy and RaTEScore, demonstrating a marked reduction in medical hallucinations. These findings indicate that Med-Collab provides an extensible, transparent, and clinically compliant approach to medical decision-making. 

Keywords: Multi-Agents · Clinical Diagnosis · Causal Reasoning

These shortcomings are particularly acute in internet hospital settings, where patients present with complex, unstructured clinical records that demand rigorous differential reasoning [25,20] rather than pattern-matched responses. Recent multi-agent frameworks [12,11,16,2,3,30] have advanced beyond single-model pipelines by distributing diagnostic reasoning across specialized agents. Nevertheless, existing systems [28,18,24] exhibit a critical structural limitation: they treat diagnosis as a collection of independent, associational outputs, identi-fying diseases correlated with symptoms without distinguishing correlation from causation. Prior work [4,33,23] has shown that this inability to separate as-sociation from causation can lead to suboptimal or even dangerous diagnostic conclusions. Consequently, generated diagnoses often remain etiologically discon-nected [6,13], failing to capture the pathological progression linking conditions (e.g., Trauma → Rib Fracture → Lung Hemorrhage → Anemia), while agent assertions lack traceable, case-specific grounding for logical auditing [27]. To address these limitations, we propose MedCollab, a causal-driven multi-agent framework that emulates the hierarchical consultation workflow of modern hospitals. We implement a structured Issue-Based Information System (IBIS) ar-gumentation protocol [8,15] requiring every diagnostic position to be grounded in traceable evidence, transforming consultation into a formally verifiable argumen-tation graph. MedCollab further constructs a Hierarchical Disease Causal Chain (HDCC) that models pathological progression through explicit causal ( →) and comorbidity ( +) relations [23], with a GP-led consensus mechanism that itera-tively penalizes logically inconsistent agents to ensure the final diagnosis reflects clinically sound reasoning. (3) Experiments on ClinicalBench and MIMIC-IV demonstrate that Med-Collab significantly outperforms leading LLMs and medical agents in diagnostic accuracy and logical coherence, with the latter quantified through RaTEScore. ## 2 Methodology: MedCollab 

Overview. As shown in Fig. 2, we first introduce dynamic agent recruitment as the foundational step (Sec. 2.1). We represent the diagnostic reasoning pro-cess using an IBIS protocol to generate structured, verifiable diagnostic positions, with each agent’s diagnosis modeled through a combination of traceable evidence and arguments (Sec. 2.2). We design a HDCC to address the logical inconsis-tency of flat predictions and capture pathological progression (Sec. 2.3). Finally, we optimize the framework through a General Practitioner (GP)-led consensus mechanism that evaluates logic and adjusts agent weights (Sec. 2.4). 

2.1 Preliminaries: Dynamic Agent Recruitment 

Agent recruitment follows a multi-agent framework in a virtual hospital set-ting. Given a patient’s clinical case S containing the unstructured chief com-plaint, medical history, and raw examination findings, a GP agent performs a dynamic evaluation to select a subset of relevant clinical specialists and exami-nation agents A = {A1, A 2, ..., A n}.Instead of enforcing rigid data structuring, the framework leverages the nat-ural language understanding capabilities of agents to establish evidence priors. The recruited examination agents directly interpret the raw medical findings to generate professional examination reports. These reports, alongside the patient’s foundational clinical record, collectively constitute the Evidence Base E. This ev-idence base provides a factual prior that constrains downstream specialists and reduces the risk of hallucinated patient information during reasoning. 4 Y. Zhan et al. 

2.2 IBIS-Structured Argumentation for Diagnostic Reasoning 

To reconstruct a rigorous diagnostic process, we define diagnostic outputs within the IBIS framework, controlling their positions and evidence. Inspired by design rationale, each agent’s diagnosis Ai is computed from structured tuples rather than free-form text. Modeling the diagnostic trajectory of every agent for irrel-evant cases would add unnecessary computational overhead and noise. Instead, we adopt an efficiency-driven exit strategy to prune the agent pool. If the case falls outside a specialist’s expertise, the agent applies the following truncation: Output (Ai) = ∅, if Domain (S) ∩ Domain (Ai) = ∅. (1) If the case falls within its domain, the agent must output its differential and preliminary diagnoses as a structured IBIS tuple Di = {(Pk, Arg k, E k) | k ∈

1...K }. This tuple is defined through four components. 

Issue ( I) represents the implicit clinical problem, namely the illness of the patient under investigation. 

Position ( Pk) denotes a proposed diagnostic hypothesis, such as “Rib Fracture”. 

Argument ( Arg k) provides the clinical rationale that explicitly connects the position with the evidence. 

Evidence ( Ek) consists of traceable facts strictly sourced from the Evidence Base E (from Sec. 2.1) or recognized medical knowledge bases. This structured design transforms informal multi-agent dialogue into a formally verifiable di-rected acyclic argumentation graph. 

2.3 Hierarchical Disease Causal Chain (HDCC) Construction 

Flat diagnostic labels often provide detailed isolated information but lack eti-ological consistency between symptoms, which can lead to illogical treatment plans. A potential solution is to map diseases into a chronological network. Drawing inspiration from pathological progression, we note that the rela-tive order and causality of diseases remain crucial. We propose the Hierarchical Disease Causal Chain to aggregate all validated positions P = S Pk.We define two clinical relations: Causality ( →) and Comorbidity ( +). The framework links diagnostic points across different stages, producing structured trajectories of disease. A candidate causal chain Cj is defined as a directed pathological progression of arbitrary length: 

Cj = P1 → P2 → · · · → PLj , (2) where Lj denotes the number of diagnostic positions in the chain. (e.g., Trauma 

→ Rib Fracture → Lung Hemorrhage → Anemia). This explicit modeling allows the system to differentiate between root causes and downstream complications. The confidence score of a chain, Score (Cj ), is computed as a weighted combi-nation of these basis positions. ## 3 Experiments and Results 

3.1 Experimental Setup Datasets and Data Processing. We evaluate MedCollab on two clinical datasets: (1) ClinicalBench [28], containing 1,500 real-world clinical cases, and (2) MIMIC-IV [14], from which we curated a subset of 595 cases. To ensure cross-dataset consistency, we reformatted the MIMIC-IV records to align with the ClinicalBench consultation schema. Each case was selected through a multi-dimensional scoring system that verifies detail richness, key findings, and struc-tural completeness. We used DeepSeek-V3 with carefully designed prompting to synthesize standardized ground truths, ensuring a strict separation between objective clinical findings and subjective impressions. 

Evaluation Metrics. We assess model performance across two primary dimen-sions: diagnostic precision and clinical reasoning quality. For diagnostic precision, we evaluate triage routing using Department Classi-fication Accuracy (DCA) and monitor hallucination suppression via Medical Fac-tual Consistency (Entity-F1) [19]. The core diagnostic performance is measured by Accuracy (ACC), which assesses whether the primary diagnosis pi generated by the system matches the normalized ground truth Gi, 1:

Moving beyond flat labels, we assess the logical coherence of generated reports across four structured sections: Diagnostic Basis (DB), Differential Diagnosis (DD), Therapeutic Principle (TP), and Treatment Plan (TX). Standard lexical overlap metrics such as BLEU and ROUGE-L are reported for textual similar-ity, but they often fail to capture medical synonyms or critical negations. We therefore prioritize RaTEScore [32], an entity-aware semantic metric designed to assess causal logic and clinical truthfulness in reasoning chains.

3.3 Ablation Study 

To evaluate the contribution of each core component, we conducted an ablation study on ClinicalBench and MIMIC-IV, with results presented in Table 2. 

Impact of Logic Auditing. Removing the Logic Auditing mechanism causes the largest degradation, with ACC dropping from 76.9% to 49.7% on Clini-calBench, confirming that the GP-led logic auditing mechanism is essential for resolving inter-specialist conflicts and filtering logically flawed reasoning chains. 

Impact of Causal Chain. Excluding the Hierarchical Disease Causal Chain reduces ACC from 76.9% to 52.9% and DB RaTEScore from 62.0% to 51.7%, demonstrating that causal modeling serves a dual role: it imposes pathological coherence on generated reports while providing the reasoning scaffold necessary for accurate diagnosis. 

## 4 Conclusion 

We presented MedCollab , a causal-driven multi-agent framework that navi-gates full-cycle clinical diagnosis through an IBIS-based argumentation proto-col and an HDCC that transforms flattened LLM predictions into pathologi-cally coherent reasoning models. Experiments on ClinicalBench and MIMIC-IV demonstrate significant improvements over leading models and medical agents in diagnostic accuracy and clinical consistency. Ablation results confirm that both the Logic Auditing mechanism and HDCC are essential for mitigating halluci-nations and ensuring logical coherence, establishing MedCollab as a reliable and interpretable pathway for AI-assisted clinical decision-making.
