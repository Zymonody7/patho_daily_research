ABSTRACT 1

From soil to the gut, communities composed of thousands of microbes perform functions such as 2

carbon sequestration and immune system regulation. Here, we introduce a data-driven approach 3

that explains how community function can be traced to just a few groups of microbes or genes. In 4

gut communities, our neural-network based clustering algorithm correctly recovers known functional 5

groups. In the ocean metagenome, it distills ∼500 gene modules down to three sparse groups 6

highlighting survival strategies at different depths. In soils, it distills ∼4400 bacterial species into two 7

groups that enter a mathematical model of nitrate metabolism. By combining interpretable ML with 8

strain isolation and sequencing experiments, we connect the metabolic specialization of each group 9

to community-wide responses to perturbations. This integrated approach yields simple structure-10 

function maps of microbiomes, allowing the discovery of molecular mechanisms underlying human 11 

and environmental health. More broadly, we illustrate how to do function-informed dimensionality 12 

reduction in biology. 13

INTRODUCTION 14 

Bacteria account for ∼10% of all biomass on earth [1]. Collectively, they perform important 15 

metabolic functions such as transferring gigatons of carbon [2–4] and nitrogen [5] between the 16 

soil and the atmosphere every year, clearing pollutants from wastewater [6], and protecting against 17 

pathogen colonization [7]. These functions arise from the concerted metabolic activity of structured 18 

communities consisting of hundreds or thousands of individual species. This complexity makes the 19 

corresponding structure-function mapping difficult to decipher. 20 

However, the biochemistry of metabolism suggests that the composition of a bacterial community 21 

and its relationship to function can be described in terms of just a few functional groups that perform 22 

distinct roles [8–15]. How can we distill these groups automatically from data and mechanistically 23 

relate them to community function? 24 

As an example, consider the microbiome function triggered when you put fertilizer in damp 25 

soil (Fig. 1a). The metabolic process of denitrification, responsible for significant greenhouse gas 26 

emissions, rapidly ensues [16, 17]. The greenhouse gas nitrous oxide (N 2O) is emitted by the 27 

collective action of functional groups, each specialized in specific steps of the metabolic cascade, 28 

e.g. conversion of NO 3 (fertilizer) to N 2O or of N 2O to N 2 (Fig. 1b) [18]. Identifying functional 29 

groups, and their role in the community, has traditionally required extensive experimental studies 30 

of isolates and their interactions [19, 20]. 31 

Here, we introduce a machine-learning algorithm called SCiFI that discovers functional groups 32 

from data, yielding interpretable structure-function maps for complex microbiomes (Fig. 1c). A33 

key finding is that each functional group contains only a few relevant members. By experimentally 34 

targeting this subset of bacterial species we can rapidly evaluate the biological mechanisms defining 35 

each group’s role in community function (Fig. 1d). In soil, this integrated machine-learning and 36 

experimental approach reveals how the members of two genetically distinct groups collectively 37 

determine denitrification dynamics across varying environments. 38 

3RESULTS 39 

A machine learning approach to function-informed clustering: the SCiFI algorithm 40 

Our goal is to distill a small number of functional groups from abundance measurements of thou-41 

sands of microbial species in a way that is informed by community function. The task of aggregating 42 

species into groups is an instantiation of a more general one called dimensionality reduction: obtain-43 

ing a low-dimensional representation of a complex system that retains its salient features. Unlike 44 

physical systems that are often representable by a single set of collective variables governing their 45 

behavior (e.g. P , V , T for a gas), biological systems do not typically possess a unique decomposition 46 

into collective variables. In proteins, for instance, the groups of residues governing thermal stability 47 

are distinct from those governing catalytic activity [21] while in cells, the genes that control dif-48 

ferentiation into fibroblasts are not those controlling differentiation into neurons [22]. Similarly, in 49 

microbiomes the groups relevant for denitrification [19] differ from those relevant for methanogenesis 50 

[23]. Nevertheless, with few exceptions [24, 25], dimensionality reduction in microbiomes is typically 51 

attempted without explicitly linking groups and function [26–28]. Species are first clustered into 52 

groups via statistical or genomic methods, and only later the resulting groups are correlated with 53 

function [29–31]. 54 

Here, we introduce a machine learning algorithm that simultaneously identifies groups and as-55 

sociates them with collective function. Our soft clustering function-informed (SCiFI) algorithm 56 

exploits a Gumbel softmax trick that turns cluster identity (typically a categorical variable) into 57 

a continuous (i.e., soft) variable (Methods). This continuous representation, beyond enabling the 58 

model to express uncertainty, is fully differentiable. Hence, optimization can be performed with 59 

gradient descent which allows information to flow, via gradients, from function to clustering. This 60 

crucial feature is what allows SCiFI to find groups that are informative of specific functional pro-61 

cesses. 62 

Figure 2a describes SCiFI in a nutshell, emphasizing how it simultaneously yields the group 63 

identification (left) and the structure-function map (right). The input is community structure, 64 

represented as a species abundance vector (gray). The output is a prediction of function (blue), 65 

which may be a vector (e.g. a time series of metabolite concentration) or a scalar (e.g. the concen-66 

tration at a single time point). To go from input to output, we first group species by multiplying 67 

the abundance vector with an interpretable clustering matrix: each row of this matrix encodes 68 

by construction the group assignment for one species (Methods). This aggregation of species by 69 

summation assumes that members within a group are indistinguishable. In systems with a large 70 

number of species many are irrelevant for function prediction. In such cases, we encourage SCiFI 71 

to find sparse groups, i.e. comprising only a few species, with an optional gating step achieved via 72 

regularization and presented in details in Methods. The resulting group abundances are in turn 73 

fed to a neural network (green) that maps them to function. The neural network enables SCiFI to 74 

accommodate nonlinear structure-function maps which, as we show below, occur in real systems. 75 

SCiFI updates both the clustering matrix and the neural network parameters (blue arrows) to mini-76 

mize the deviation between predicted and true function. Simultaneous updating is the architectural 77 

feature that ensures feedback between clustering and structure-function mapping during training. 78 

This is how SCiFI’s groups become function informed. 79 

We benchmark SCiFI using a simulated dataset (Methods) where function is generated from a 80 

small number of groups through a nonlinear structure-function map (Fig. 2b). Nonlinearity is a 81 

typical feature of the experimental microbiome datasets we analyze below. For benchmarking we 82 

consider other models that can be used to find functional groups. Each of these models lack one 83 

or both of the SCiFI’s defining features, namely its nonlinearity and function-informed clustering 84

SCiFI algorithm correctly identifies functional groups for in vitro gut microbiomes 97 

We validate SCiFI’s performance on synthetic gut microbiome experiments from Ref. [32], which 98 

form communities consisting of combinations of 30 gut bacteria. This dataset consists of hundreds 99 

of paired measurements of strain abundances and fermentation products relevant for gut health 100 

including butyrate and succinate. 101 

We first aim to identify functional groups relevant for butyrate production. The correct number 102 

of groups is a priori unknown. To find it, we train multiple networks over a range of possible group 103 

numbers Ngroups and see when the error in butyrate prediction stops decreasing (Fig. 3a). Because 104 

the number of species is small, we do not require SCiFI’s optional gating step. We find that four 105 

functional groups are necessary to accurately predict butyrate production of the whole community. 106 

The groups we learn are robust (SI Figs. S6, S7) and interpretable in terms of the phenotypes de-107 

scribed in Ref. [32] (Fig. 3b and SI Fig. S8). The first group contains a single member, Anaerostipes 108 

caccae (AC) a butyrate producer which can switch between low butyrate (sugar-utilizing butyrate 109 

production) and high butyrate (lactate-utilizing butyrate production) producing states depending 110 

on environmental pH [32]. This explains the second group of so-called “pH buffers”, which are species 111 

that are highly correlated with pH that modulate butyrate production by impacting AC’s mode of 112 

production (SI Fig. S9a). The third group contains the rest of the direct butyrate producers, while 113 

the final group contains the remaining species which are not important for butyrate production. 114 

These groups, which were independently experimentally confirmed in Ref. [32] show that SCiFI 115 

correctly learns functional groups in the microbiome. 116 

Knowledge of these four coarse-grained groups suffices to accurately predict butyrate concentra-117 

tion (Fig. 3c). This is possible only because SCiFI can learn non-linear structure-function relation-118 

ships. If the non-linearity is removed and we instead use linear regression to predict butyrate from 119 

the (learned) group abundances, the model suffers a significant decrease in accuracy (Fig. 3d and 120 

SI Fig. S10). 121 

Repeating our analysis using succinate concentration instead of butyrate results in a completely 122 

different group structure (Fig. 3e-f). SCiFI correctly ignores the dominant species (measured by 123 

average abundance) AC, and instead isolates strains from the Bacteroidetes phylum. In contrast 124 

to the butyrate case, the loss continues to see modest decreases as we increase the number of 125 

groups beyond the initial plateau (at Ncluster = 2 ) which correspond to subsequent subdivisions of 126

7How can we understand how the learned groups contribute to community function? To move 128 

forward we use the learned groups as the starting point for a genomic analysis. Namely, we use 129 

sequenced genomes of group members to search for key enzymes that produce succinate using the 130 

Biocyc database [33]. This reveals that all nine strains assigned to the putative succinate-producing 131 

group (brown in Fig. 3f) have at least one copy of the fumarate reductase gene. By contrast, all 132 

other strains lack fumarate reductase except for the two species EL and FP, which have low average 133 

abundance (SI Fig. S9b). 134 

In contrast to the butyrate case, the relationship between the functional groups and succinate 135 

prediction is simple: a linear model predicts succinate from the group abundances nearly as well 136 

as the neural network (Fig. 3g; green in Fig. 3h). However, this is only true when the clustering is 137 

function-informed. If we instead project the abundance data onto its principal components (blue) 138 

as opposed to our function-informed clustering matrix, a linear model is incapable of predicting 139 

succinate concentration. This indicates that the dominant variance in abundances does not re-140 

flect changes in succinate concentration and highlights the importance of identifying clusters in a 141 

function-informed manner. 142 

In summary, because it is function-informed, SCiFI can identify multiple different groupings 143 

from the same set of abundance data. Further, the groups that we learn have clearly interpretable 144 

biological roles, and in the case of succinate they also possess a distinct genetic signature. 145 

SCiFI finds functional groups in natural ocean and soil microbiomes. 146 

In contrast to the synthetic communities above, in natural ocean and soil samples the experimen-147 

talist has little to no control over the composition of the community. Our method identifies relevant 148 

functional groups even in these settings. 149 

First, we aim to find functional groups in the ocean microbiome using the Tara Oceans dataset 150 

[34, 35]. In this data we lack access to the functional outputs of the community: we cannot 151 

measure how much nitrate is being dynamically consumed, only the steady state concentration. We 152 

nevertheless treat the environmental variables as a proxy for functional measurements, and train our 153 

networks to predict sample nitrate concentrations. Two groups suffice to accurately predict nitrate, 154 

and we find that the structure-function map is well-approximated by a linear model (Fig. 3i-l and 155 

SI Fig. S10). Because we lack a ground truth in this case, we confirm that our learned functional 156 

groups contain all strains identified by an alternative method (Ensemble Quotient Optimization; 157 

EQO) introduced in Ref. [24]. Like EQO, SCiFI produces a group consisting of “complementary” 158 

(anti-correlated) species, but in contrast to EQO it does so without having this feature built in the 159 

objective function (SI Fig. S8). 160 

Finally, we consider soil microcosm experiments from Ref. [36] which measure nitrate reduction 161 

dynamics together with the end-point phyla abundances under a range of pH perturbations. In 162 

contrast to the previous examples, function is a vector representing the concentration of nitrate over 163 

time. Our method indicates that three functional groups, which in particular isolate Proteobacteria 164 

and Bacillota, are necessary to predict nitrate dynamics (Fig. 3m-p). This is consistent with previous 165 

work showing the key role of members of these phyla in governing denitrification in soils subjected 166 

to basic perturbations [36]. These groups are sufficient to predict nitrate consumption only if the 167 

structure-function map is nonlinear (Fig. 3o-p and SI Fig. S10). 168

SCiFI distills groups of genes in the ocean metagenome 169 

Nothing about SCiFI limits its applicability to clustering species – it just as readily clusters genes. 170 

We now demonstrate this capability by finding relevant groups of genes present in microbial com-171 

munities in the world’s oceans. The Tara Oceans dataset [34] provides measurements of gene 172 

abundances, which we consider aggregated at the level of KEGG modules [37, 38], and environmen-173 

tal conditions at ∼ 100 sites across the global ocean (Fig. 4a). The large number of gene modules 174 

(∼500) poses a challenge to interpreting the resulting groups because even if there are a small num-175 

ber of groups, they will be composed of a large number of genes. Here, we employ SCiFI’s optional 176 

gating procedure to achieve an approximate twenty-fold reduction in the number of relevant genes, 177 

which facilitates interpretation of their individual roles. 178 

Similarly, nothing about SCiFI limits its applicability to clustering based on function alone – 179 

it can also incorporate the role of abiotic forces. The Tara data includes abiotic factors known to 180 

structure marine microbiomes, such as temperature [35]. To account for the impact of this key 181 

abiotic factor in metabolic function, we ask SCiFI to predict temperature along with oxygen and 182 

9nitrate concentrations. Three groups suffice to make accurate predictions (Fig. 4b; SI Fig. S11). 183 

Two groups are strongly correlated to nitrate concentration (blue; group 1) and temperature (red; 184 

group 3) (Fig. 4c), while the third (green; group 2) shows clear depletion at the transition to the 185 

oxygen minimum zone. 186 

After the sparsity promoting (gating) step, each group consists of only a small number of gene 187 

modules Fig. 4d (also SI Figs. S12, S13). This allows us to interpret how the enrichment of groups 188 

at certain depths (Fig. 4b) corresponds to different survival strategies. For example, group 1, which 189 

is abundant in deep waters, contains modules for respiration with alternative electron acceptors 190 

(nitrogen, sulfur). More interestingly, it also contains modules related to degradation of pyrimidine, 191 

histidine, and tryptophan. This suggests that the bacteria residing in the nutrient poor conditions 192 

of the deep ocean survive by scavenging nucleotides and amino acids for energy and growth. Group 193 

3, in contrast, contains several gene modules related to protective compounds such as pigments 194 

(beta-Carotene), protective vitamins (ascorbate) and exopolysaccharide (uronate pathway), a key 195 

component of biofilms [39, 40]. These compounds might reflect adaptations to the high stress that 196 

surface bacteria experience at the surface due to UV radiation [41] or phage predation [42]. 197 

SCiFI learns functional groups that are dynamical variables of mathematical models 198 

Up to now, we have interpreted the functional groups learned by SCiFI by looking at the phenotypes 199 

of group members, their phylogeny, or in the case of clustering metagenomes, the function of proteins 200 

those genes encode. However, this analysis does not reveal how the groups work together to drive 201 

community function since the structure-function map is encoded by a neural network that is not 202 

readily interpretable. We now show that the functional groups learned by SCiFI can be directly 203 

used as variables in a fully interpretable mathematical model that relates groups to community 204 

function. 205 

As an example, we consider how nitrate utilization in soil microbiomes is impacted by short 206 

and long-term changes in soil pH, a key environmental variable [36, 43]. Previous work has shown 207 

that a consumer-resource model predicts the dynamics of nitrate utilization (the resource) from 208 

a single effective biomass (the consumer) [36]. The model parameters, including the size of the 209 

effective biomass, were inferred from the nitrate measurements alone. The approach revealed broad 210 

mechanisms of pH induced changes in function, but did not provide a principled way to identify the 211 

relevant bacteria from sequencing measurements. Here we show that SCiFI solves this problem. 212 

We train SCiFI to predict trajectories of nitrate concentration using a vector of 4395 amplicon 213 

sequence variant (ASV) abundances as input. This data comes from experiments which measure 214

11 SCiFI guides targeted experiments that reveal biological mechanisms 233 

The two functional groups we find exhibit distinct dynamical behaviors (Fig. 5b). What is the 234 

biological explanation for this difference? SCiFI by itself does not answer this question. However, 235 

it does perform the first step of an integrated pipeline that combines machine learning with exper-236 

iments to reveal underlying biological mechanisms. Concretely, our approach exploits the sparse 237 

groups learned by SCiFI to guide targeted experiments on representative species. 238 

For nitrate reduction in soil, we seek a genetic explanation. This choice is motivated by the 239 

fact that genes encode nitrate utilization traits of individual species [45]. We isolated a dominant 240 

strain from each group ( Neobacillus fumarioli from Group 1; Peribacillus simplex from Group 2) 241 

and sequenced their genomes (Methods). These strains were selected because they were among the 242 

most abundant in their respective groups. Because these strains differ only at the genus level, the 243 

distinction between them would not have been discovered without function-informed clustering. 244 

Whole genome sequencing of these isolates revealed that, despite their taxonomic proximity, 245 

these strains differ in the denitrification enzymes they harbor (Fig. 6a). While the Neobacillus 246 

strain (blue) possesses a complete set of genes to reduce nitrate to dinitrogen, the Peribacillus strain 247 

(red) can only perform the intermediate step of reducing nitrite to nitric oxide. In Figure 6b we 248 

show the inferred genomes of all group members (PiCRUSt2, [44]) sorted by their average absolute 249 

abundance (right). We observe the same pattern: the most abundant Group 1 strains (blue) often 250 

have a full set of genes required to convert nitrate to dinitrogen, while Group 2 strains perform only 251 

partial steps in the pathway (Fig. 6b). 252 

This finding allows us to explain how the nitrate reduction capacity of a community changes in 253 

response to pH perturbations. In growth-inhibited conditions we observe that acidic soils (pH ≲ 6;254 

gray in Fig. 6c) show robust nitrate reduction which is constant over a range of pH perturbations, 255 

while neutral soils (pH ≳ 6.5; green in Fig. 6c) are more pH-sensitive. These two soil types differ 256 

also in their composition: Group 1 dominates acidic soils, while Group 2 dominates neutral soils 257 

(Fig. 6d). 258 

Taken together, these observations suggest the following mechanism to explain community sen-259 

sitivity to pH perturbations. Neutral soils are characterized by a large population of Group 2, 260 

which reduces nitrate by splitting the pathway among several different constituent strains. Such 261 

cross-feeding communities are susceptible to nitrite toxicity at low pH [46], which slows the overall 262 

utilization of nitrate (Fig 6e, left). Acidic soils, by contrast, are dominated by Group 1 which is 263 

expected to fully reduce nitrate to dinitrogen and relieve nitrite toxicity, resulting in robustness to 264 

pH perturbations (Fig. 6e, right). Both behaviors persist in growth conditions (SI Fig. S17). Our 265 

integrated pipeline reveals how collective function depends on the differential response of individual 266 

groups to perturbations. 267

13 community function. Enrichment cultures necessarily remove microbes from their natural context 278 

and hence do not capture how a microbe behaves when embedded in a complex community, while 279 

genomics does not consider metabolite fluxes explicitly. 280 

Our approach is not subject to these shortcomings because it learns functional groups directly 281 

from data of natural communities. This success hinges on the fact that SCiFI is function-informed 282 

and nonlinear. Because it is function-informed, we do not need experiments to be fine-tuned to 283 

elicit the growth of one particular functional group. Instead, groups can be identified post hoc from 284 

any experiment that generates correlations between abundances and function. Nonlinearity allows 285 

SCiFI to capture interactions between groups, such as those between A. caccae and pH buffering 286 

strains that modulate modes of butyrate production (Fig. 3a-d). 287 

SCiFI can dramatically simplify the process of characterizing the structure of the community and 288 

its relationship to function. Because it is function-informed, SCiFI can leverage a single experiment 289 

that measures multiple functions to learn multiple groupings from the same abundance data. This 290 

could dramatically reduce the number of experiments needed to characterize the structure of a 291 

community. 292 

Equation learning pipelines that build on SCiFI can explain the relationship between structure 293 

and function. Here, we used SCiFI to extract the relevant variables for a consumer resource model 294 

whose mathematical form was chosen based on intuition grounded in previous work [36]. However, 295 

if we replace the neural network structure-function map with a sparse-regression model [47] or a 296 

NeuralODE [48], the form of the mathematical model itself can be inferred directly from data with 297 

little a priori knowledge. 298 

Complex structure-function relationships are not unique to microbiomes. Our integrated pipeline 299 

can find low-dimensional descriptions of structure in other contexts with no change to the core algo-300 

rithm. The underlying assumption behind SCiFI is that group members (of which bacterial species 301 

are but an example) are indistinguishable and may meaningfully be aggregated by summation. Any 302 

problem that has this feature may be tackled with our framework. We suggest two cases where this 303 

assumption is likely to be fulfilled. First, SCiFI may be applied to neural firing rates to learn the 304 

low-dimensional “neural manifold” underlying motor functions [49]. For example, a simple aggrega-305 

tion of (preprocessed) neural activity has been shown to predict the direction of rhesus monkey arm 306 

movement from single-neuron data alone [50]. SCiFI could automate this aggregation procedure 307 

and extend it to non-linear structure-function mapping. As a second example, SCiFI may be used 308 

to understand the structure-function relationship between T-cell receptor sequence and immune 309 

response. The immune response to a particular antigen is determined by the aggregated activity 310 

of all T-cells which recognize it [51]. SCiFI could learn functional groups from experiments which 311 

challenge a T-cell community with a complex pathogen (e.g. a whole virus) and measure their 312 

collective response (e.g. total interferon γ production). 313 

LIMITATIONS OF THE STUDY 314 

SCiFI identifies groups based on a statistical relationship between abundances and function. How-315 

ever, it is not guaranteed that these groups are actually generators of function. This is a general 316 

limitation of machine learning methods that can be ultimately traced to the fact that correlation 317 

is not causation. While in every dataset we considered SCiFI learned biologically relevant groups, 318 

this outcome ultimately needs to be independently checked either with mathematical modeling or 319 

further experiments. 320 

As a data-driven approach, SCiFI inevitably inherits the limitations of the dataset. Noise can 321 

hinder SCiFI’s ability to find groups, especially groups that only weakly impact function. For 322 

14 example, in a simulated linear degradation cascade where function is defined as the concentration 323 

of the final product, groups that are far upstream are recovered imperfectly even though they are 324 

biologically necessary (SI Fig. S4). 325 

Finally, as described above, the grouping sought by SCiFI inherently assumes that species within 326 

a group are indistinguishable. While this limits the class of problems to which it may be applied, it is 327 

also crucial for the interpretability of our method. SCiFI may be adapted to allow for distinguishable 328 

group members in a way analogous to how we introduced the gating to enforce sparsity. However, 329 

this extension may come at the cost of reduced interpretability. 330

We extract a “consensus” clustering as follows. First, we sort the ensemble of models based on their predictive accuracy ( R2 of their function predictions versus the true predictions). Next, we choose a subset of high-performing models, typically the top 25% to 50%. In SI Fig. S15 we show how in the soil data, the consensus grouping is insensitive to the choice of how many top-performing models we take. We then make the consensus grouping based on majority rule: i.e. if five ensemble members assign species i to group 1 and four assign it to group 2, then the consensus assignment will be to group 1. When gating is used, the above procedure is modified slightly by adding the additional condition that species are only included in the consensus grouping if they are not gated in a majority of ensemble members. Clusterings are only unique up to a permutation invariance which we must account for. In other words, the groups {1, 2, 3} for one model may correspond to the groups {2, 3, 1} in another model just because of the arbitrariness of the group labeling. We correct for this by reordering groups based on their average abundance, or, in the case of the ocean metagenome, based on their correlation coefficient with nitrate.

A and C. When one of these is depleted, growth ceases so that x1 =const, x2=const, and A decays linearly in time. We compare the highlighted distributions to the model error using the “correct” groups (shown as vertical dashed line) by computing the p-value shown in the upper right corner. We find that shuffling either of the biomasses individually or both together leads to significantly worse performance than the true model (bottom row). However, shuffling biomass 2 only, while still worse than the true model, degrades performance to a lesser extent than the other two randomization procedures (horizontal arrow in bottom row). There is also almost no effect on the prediction of biomass 1 (arrow in top row). Shuffling biomass 1 significantly degrades predictive power for both nitrate dynamics and biomass 1 end-point abundance. However, the performance in biomass 2 prediction actually increases (arrow in middle row), possibly hinting that it could not be as accurately predicted because of a trade-off in the model’s ability to predict both biomass 1 and biomass 2. Together, these results suggest that biomass 2 is not as important as biomass 1 in making predictions of nitrate dynamics or biomass 1 dynamics. 

Microbiome datasets 

> Human gut synthetic community dataset

Synthetic community experiments with human gut microbial strains were done by Clark et al. 

(2021), where they inoculated 1850 different combinations of 26 representative gut strains in the media in the anaerobic chamber to measure the final concentrations of produced butyrate, succinate, acetate, and lactate. For each synthetic community, the relative abundance of the endpoint commu-nity was measured by 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing as described by the original authors [32]. We used the endpoint relative abundance of strains as compositional input and produced butyrate and succinate concentrations as functional output for training the neural network. The gene annotation information of each strain’s genome (Biocyc database) from the original authors [33] was used to validate the functional clusters by enzyme presence or absence. The dimension (sample × strain) of the compositional data was 1850 ×26. The functional data used for neural network prediction had a dimension (sample ×metabolite concentration) of 1850 ×1, respectively for butyrate and succinate. 

> Tara Oceans natural community dataset

136 Tara Oceans samples were sampled by the original authors [34, 35] across the oceans at different depths. For each sample, they measured nitrate concentration, temperature, and dissolved oxygen level (6 samples have NaN values for at least one of these measurements and are omitted), which we used as functional outputs to train the neural network. The relative abundance of taxa in each sample was derived from 16S rDNA fragments in the metagenomic sequencing (miTAGs) as 21 described by the original authors [34, 35] and Shan et al [24]. The dimension (sample ×taxa) of the compositional data was 136 ×97 at the genus level. For the gene module abundance of each sample, we used the annotated gene tables uploaded by the original authors [34, 35]. The dimension (sample ×gene) of the gene composition data was 136 ×515 for module-level annotation. The functional data used for neural network prediction had a dimension (sample × environmental variable) of 136 ×1 for the case we identify functional groupings with nitrate concentrations or 130 ×3 for the case we use all 3 environmental variables (nitrate concentration, temperature, and dissolved oxygen level) to identify functional groups, as we removed 6 samples without corresponding oxygen measurements. 

> Soil pH perturbation dataset

20 Topsoils were collected across a native pH gradient (4.7–8.32) in the Cook Agronomy Farm (CAF) in the Long-Term Agroecosystem Research (LTAR) (Pullman, WA, USA) and perturbed each soil slurry into 13 different pH levels as well as adding 2mM nitrate to initiate denitrification in 1:2 soil-to-water slurries under anaerobic conditions, as described previously. [36]. To infer indigenous biomass activity of nitrate reduction, we included chloramphenicol-treated (CHL+) controls, in which protein synthesis and growth were inhibited, yielding linear nitrate reduction dynamics. In contrast, untreated controls (CHL–) allowed microbial growth and physiological responses to the imposed pH conditions. For functional outputs, we measured the functional dynamics (10 time points during the 4-day incubation) of nitrate, nitrite, and ammonium concentrations. The absolute abundance of each taxon per sample was acquired by dividing reads from 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing with reads from the internal standard of known spiked-in strains [36]. 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing was performed on endpoint perturbed samples derived from 10 of the 20 CAF sampled soils. We removed 18 samples lacking complete triplicate CHL+/CHL- pairs in Soil12 (perturbed unit 20 and 40) and Soil15 (perturbed unit 12). We removed taxa that had fewer than 1,000 total reads across the entire sequencing dataset to minimize noise and sparsity coming from very low-abundance taxa. The dimensions (sample × taxa) of the compositional data were 762 ×28 for phyla, 762 ×256 for family, and 762 ×4395 for ASV level. The functional data used for neural network prediction had a dimension (sample × metabolite dynamics) of 762 ×10 for nitrate, 762 ×20 for nitrate and nitrite, 762 ×30 for nitrate, nitrite, and ammonium. 

> Strain isolation and whole genome sequencing

For isolation, as described previously [36], we thawed endpoint slurry samples from soil pH pertur-bation experiments stored in 25% glycerol in -80 ◦C, streaked onto 1/10 × tryptic soy agar (TSA) plates and 1/10x Reasoner’s 2A (R2A) plates adjusted to the endpoint pH of each slurry sample. Plates were incubated at 30 ◦C and checked for growth every 24 hours. Genomic DNA was extracted from pure colonies using the DNeasy Ultraclean Microbial Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Unique strains were identified by Sanger sequencing of the 16S rRNA region amplified by 27F and 1492R primers, followed by BLAST against the NCBI 16S rRNA reference sequences for taxonomic identi-fication. We decided to further investigate the genetic composition of the two strains taxonomically identified as Neobacillus fumarioli (Group 1 representative strain) and Peribacillus simplex (Group 2 representative strain) with whole genome sequencing. For whole-genome sequencing, approximately 5 × 10 9 cells were preserved in DNA/RNA Shield (Zymo Research) and submitted to Plasmidsaurus (USA). Genomic DNA was prepared using an amplification-free library construction protocol (ONT V14 chemistry) and sequenced on Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT) R10.4.1 flow cells. Raw signals were basecalled using the Dorado 22 Super-Accurate (SUP) model with a default Q10 quality filter. Read quality was further refined by removing the lowest 5 % of reads using Filtlong v0.2.1. De novo assembly was executed via the Autocycler pipeline, which generates a consensus from multiple independent assemblers, including Flye v2.9.6+, Hifiasm, and Plassembler v1.8.0+. The final consensus assembly was polished using Medaka v1.8.0 and oriented to the dnaA start position using dnaapler. Structural and functional annotation was performed using Bakta v1.11.4 utilizing the v6.0 full database. Species identification was verified through Mash v2.3 and Sourmash v4.6.1 against RefSeq and GenBank databases, while genome completeness and contamination were assessed via CheckM v1.2.2. Supplementary Figure S3: Training hyperparameters impacting performance on synthetic data. We explore how SCiFI’s ability to recover the correct groups depends on critical training param-eters and on the dimension of the input. The synthetic data follows the structure discussed in Methods and used in Fig. 2. The basic group structure is ˆc = (1 , 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 3, 3, 3, 3) which assigns 10 species to 3 clusters. We vary the dimensionality of the system while keeping the group number fixed by simply repeating ˆc for an integer number of repetitions (e.g. 10 repetitions would correspond to a dimensional-ity of 100). The neural network used to model the structure function map is small, consisting of 2 layers of 48 neurons each. This small network is sufficient because the function is piecewise linear. In all cases we measure group recovery with the Jaccard index (see Methods). (a) Variation of group recovery as a function of dimensionality for different values of dropout. Every faint dot corresponds to one model of an ensemble ( Nens = 15 ), slightly jiggled in the x-direction for visualization. The solid dots with connecting line are the average Jaccard index across all ensemble members. The number of samples used for training is 1000 and we see performance start to decrease at Ndim = 100 . Dropout makes negligible difference to the group recovery, but it significantly improves generalization (not shown). (b) Same as in (a) but varying the number of training samples. Performance increases significantly when the number of samples is increased. This suggests that given enough data, SCiFI should find the correct groups. (c) Same as in (c) but varying the learning rate during training. In general, learning rate can significantly impact performance. Here we see that using a larger learning rate improves group recovery. However, too large and the networks will fail to train (not shown). (d) Comparison of SCiFI group recovery to the Metropolis Hastings algorithm from Ref. [25]. Here we show the recovery of each group individually. SCiFI generically outperforms the Metropolis Hastings algorithm. Here we also see that the group recovery differs across groups. This suggests that the decrease in performance at 

Ndim ≈ 100 is due to the particular structure-function map used.
