## Discovery of Small-Molecule Inhibitors of the Pycsar Antiphage Defense

Marian A Addo et al. ACS Chem Biol.2026.

## Abstract

The increasing prevalence of multidrug-resistant bacteria has intensified the search for alternative therapeutic strategies. Bacteriophages (the natural viral predators of bacteria) are one promising solution. However, bacterial immune systems confer phage resistance, which can limit the efficacy of phage therapies. Small-molecule modulators of bacterial immunity are a promising but underexplored strategy to enhance phage therapy. We report the first small-molecule inhibitors of the Pycsar immune system, a 3',5'-cCMP signal-based defense mechanism, in bacteria. These inhibitors exhibit efficacy in _Escherichia coli_ cells, enabling phage Bas34 to overcome Pycsar-mediated resistance. The Pycsar system relies on two proteins: a CTP cyclase, which generates the cCMP signal, and a cCMP-activated transmembrane effector, which disrupts the bacterial membrane of the host cells and prevents phage reproduction. Our findings reveal that the identified inhibitors specifically target the cytotoxic effects of the transmembrane effector rather than the signal-producing cyclase. These molecules highlight the promise of chemical modulators to suppress bacterial immunity and, thereby, expand the therapeutic potential of bacteriophages against multidrug-resistant pathogens.

**Figure 1.**
High-throughput screen identifies inhibitors against Pycsar. (A) Gene composition of the E831 Pycsar system comprising cyclase, c-terminal immunoglobulin-like (AGS-C), and transmembrane helix (TM) domains. (B) Cartoon illustrating the mechanism of defense by Pycsar. (C) Pycsar E831 protection against phage Bas34 observed through phage-induced lysis in liquid culture at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.03. (D) Two-dimensional (2D) Z-score plot of a screen to identify inhibitors of E831 Pycsar. The _y_-axis measures cell density at the end of the experiment (low values indicate bacteria killing). The _x_-axis measures cell density after 3 h. Phage lysis curves reach a high initial OD 600 nm at ~3 h before dropping to low OD 600 nm values ~10 h (see panel C). Therefore, inhibitors of Pycsar (which restore phage-induced lysis) should have average Z-scores (~0) in the _x_-axis (after 3 h) and low Z-scores in the _y_-axis (after 10 h). This 2D analysis helps to exclude generally bacteriostatic or bacteriocidal compounds that would impact OD 600 nm already by 3 h and therefore exhibit low Z-scores in both axes. The colored circles in the lower right quadrant, labeled compounds 1, 2, and * display the most promising ‘hits’ from this screen. (E) Chemical structures of Pycsar inhibitors 1 and 2. (F) Phage-induced lysis curves of compound 1 treatment on Pycsar-expressing _E. coli_. (G) Phage-induced lysis curves of compound 2 treatment on Pycsar-expressing _E. coli_. (H) Bacteria growth measurement after compound 1 and 2 treatments in the absence of phage. (I) Phage replication induction by compounds 1 and 2, quantified by measuring phage plaque-forming units (PFUs) produced 15 h postinfection. In all plots, error bars are the standard error of the mean (SEM) with biological triplicates. In panel I, individual replicates are displayed with circles.

**Figure 3.**
Production of cCMP is not inhibited by compounds. (A) Extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) of cCMP [M–H]− (_m_/_z_ 304.0339 – 304.0345) present in cell lysate from PycC-expressing _E. coli_ 60 min after infection with Bas34. (B) cCMP level measured in lysates of PycC-expressing _E. coli_ 60 min following Bas34 infection of cells pretreated with 111 _μ_ M compound 1. (C) cCMP level measured in lysates of PycC-expressing _E. coli_ 60 min following Bas34 infection of cells pretreated with 33 _μ_ M compound 2. (D) Compound 1 reverts cell toxicity from an exogenous cCMP (250 _μ_ M) signal molecule. (E) Compound 2 reverts cell toxicity from an exogenous cCMP (250 _μ_ M) signal molecule. In all plots, error bars are the standard error of the mean (SEM) with biological triplicates. In panels B and C, individual replicates are displayed with circles. _P_-values were calculated using a Student _t_ test (p-values = 0.06 and 0.11 for panels B and C, respectively).

**Figure 4.**
Analogs of compounds 1 and 2 reveal the necessary moieties for Pycsar inhibition. (A) Dose–response curves evaluating “Pycsar strength,” which is the integrated area under the bacterial lysis curve (AUC) after phage infection over the range of 0.5–15 h, normalized to the positive control AUC (phage infection in complete absence of the Pycsar defense) and the negative control AUC (phage infection in Pycsar-defended cells with no inhibitor added) upon treatment with different concentrations of inhibitors 1 and 2. See Methods for more details. (B) Minimal inhibitory concentrations (MICs) of phenyl ring-modified analogs of compound 1. (C) MICs of aldimine-modified analogs of compound 1. (D) Inactive naphthalenol-modified analogs of compound 1. (E) Inactive anthraquinone and quinone analogs of compound 2. In panel (A), error bars are the standard error of the mean (SEM) with biological triplicates. In panels (B–E), MICs were defined as the lowest concentrations at which bacterial lysis by phage reduced the OD 600 nm to <0.5 at 15 h (see Figures S4–S7).
