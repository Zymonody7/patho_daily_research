## 摘要
引言：基于废水的监测（WBS）是监测社区层面病原体传播的重要流行病学工具。废水中的病毒浓度通常使用粪便指示物（如辣椒轻斑驳病毒，PMMoV RNA）进行归一化；然而，在热带城市环境中，PMMoV RNA 信号可能受到季节性降雨、雨水入渗和卫生基础设施的影响，从而可能损害其可靠性。本研究旨在表征乌干达坎帕拉 PMMoV RNA 的季节性和站点特异性变异，并评估其在热带城市条件下作为粪便指示物的适用性。方法：2023年3月至2024年5月期间，在坎帕拉大都会区的四个废水处理设施每周采集基于 Moore 拭子的进水废水样本。使用 RT-qPCR 对 PMMoV RNA 进行定量，并以每 100 毫升基因组拷贝数的对数值（log₁₀ GC/100 mL）表示。使用 Kruskal-Wallis H 检验及 Dunn 事后校正评估设施间的差异。使用 Pearson 相关系数定量 PMMoV RNA 与月平均降雨量之间的关联。拟合了一个以降雨量和站点为固定效应、站点为随机截距的线性混合模型（LMM），以将方差分解为降雨驱动源和结构源。结果：在所有四个设施的 244 个样本中，有 239 个（97.95%）检测到 PMMoV RNA。浓度表现出显著的空间和时间变异性（Kruskal-Wallis H = 9.83, p = 0.020），这主要由下水道网络结构（包括基础设施类型、水力配置和入流敏感性）驱动，而非仅由人口规模驱动（人均分析：H = 84.29, p < 0.0001）。Naalya 废水稳定塘显示出最高且最稳定的浓度（均值 = 6.09 log₁₀ GC/100 mL；IQR = 1.03），而 Bugolobi 粪便污泥处理厂显示出最低的集中趋势（均值 = 5.27）和最大的变异性（IQR = 2.60），反映了间歇性的污泥输入。PMMoV RNA 与月平均降雨量之间存在显著的强负相关（Pearson r = -0.623, p = 0.013）。LMM 证实降雨是一个独立且与站点无关的预测因子（β = -0.0077 log₁₀ GC/100 mL/mm, p < 0.001），降雨量每增加 100 mm，PMMoV RNA 浓度约降低 6 倍。结论：坎帕拉废水中的 PMMoV RNA 浓度受到降雨和集水区基础设施的系统性且大幅度的调节，如果不进行调整，季节性稀释足以使归一化病原体信号的解释产生偏差。这些发现挑战了支持基于比例归一化的比例稀释假设，并强调了在热带城市 WBS 计划中，需要将下水道网络表征作为站点选择和解释的基础，同时采用降雨调整的解释框架、站点特异性验证以及补充性粪便指示物。

## Abstract
INTRODUCTION: Wastewater-based surveillance (WBS) is a vital epidemiological tool for monitoring community-level pathogen transmission. Viral concentrations in wastewater are commonly normalized using fecal indicators such as Pepper Mild Mottle Virus (PMMoV) RNA; however, in tropical urban settings, PMMoV RNA signal may be influenced by seasonal rainfall, stormwater infiltration, and sanitation infrastructure, potentially compromising its reliability. This study aimed to characterize the seasonal and site-specific variability of PMMoV RNA in Kampala, Uganda, and to evaluate its suitability as a fecal indicator under tropical urban conditions. METHODS: Moore swab-based influent wastewater samples were collected weekly between March 2023 and May 2024 at four wastewater treatment facilities in the Kampala Metropolitan Area. PMMoV RNA was quantified using RT-qPCR and expressed as log₁₀ genomic copies per 100 mL (GC/100 mL). Between-facility differences were assessed using the Kruskal-Wallis H test with Dunn's post-hoc correction. The association between PMMoV RNA and monthly average rainfall was quantified using Pearson's correlation. A linear mixed model (LMM) with rainfall and site as fixed effects and a random intercept for site was fitted to partition the variance into rainfall-driven and structural sources. RESULTS: PMMoV RNA was detected in 239 of 244 samples (97.95%) across all four facilities. Concentrations exhibited significant spatial and temporal variability (Kruskal-Wallis H = 9.83, p = 0.020), driven by sewershed structure, including infrastructure type, hydraulic configuration, and inflow susceptibility rather than population size alone (per capita analysis: H = 84.29, p < 0.0001). Naalya wastewater stabilization pond showed the highest and most stable concentrations (mean = 6.09 log₁₀ GC/100 mL; IQR = 1.03), while Bugolobi fecal sludge treatment plant showed the lowest central tendency (mean = 5.27) and greatest variability (IQR = 2.60), reflecting intermittent sludge inputs. A strong, significant negative correlation was identified between PMMoV RNA and monthly average rainfall (Pearson r = - 0.623, p = 0.013). The LMM confirmed rainfall as an independent, site-invariant predictor (β = -0.0077 log₁₀ GC/100 mL per mm, p < 0.001), with a 100 mm increase in rainfall corresponding to an approximately 6-fold reduction in PMMoV RNA concentration. CONCLUSION: PMMoV RNA concentrations in Kampala's wastewater are systematically and substantially modulated by rainfall and catchment infrastructure, with seasonal dilution sufficient to bias the interpretation of normalized pathogen signals if left unadjusted. These findings challenge the assumption of proportional dilution underpinning ratio-based normalization and highlight the need for seweshed characterization as a foundation for site selection and interpretation, alongside rainfall-adjusted interpretation frameworks, site-specific validation, and complementary fecal indicators in tropical urban WBS programs.
