## Abstract

Pediatric health is the foundation for people's lifelong health. The co-evolution of host genetics and the gut microbiome fosters a symbiotic relationship that is important for pediatric growth and the pathogenesis of various diseases. However, a comprehensive overview of the human genetics-gut microbiome axis in pediatric diseases remains unavailable. This review summarizes the human genetic variants that are associated with pediatric diseases, affecting the nervous, respiratory, and immune systems, as well as those linked to preterm birth (PTB), as identified by genome-wide association studies (GWAS). As the gut microbiome plays a crucial role in pediatric health, we have systematically discussed microbial biomarkers associated with the onset and progression of pediatric diseases, with an emphasis on their clinical impact across four key axes: the gut-brain, gut-lung, gut-skin, and gut-immune axes. The GWAS on the gut microbiome revealed numerous genetic variants that intricately regulate its composition. These variants predispose individuals to gut microbiome dysbiosis, potentially initiating or exacerbating pediatric disease manifestations. Moreover, the underrepresentation of populations from low- and middle-income countries in existing microbiome-related data, coupled with technical challenges, limits our understanding of the association between microbiome and health. Finally, we emphasize the promising potential of elucidating and modulating host gene-gut microbiome interactions to offer novel insights for advancing precision pediatric medicine and developing innovative therapeutic strategies.

**Keywords:** GWAS; Genetics; Gut microbiome; Gut–X axis; Health; Pediatrics.

The multiple gut–organ axes in pediatric diseases. The microbiome genome-wide association studies that combine human genetic variations and the gut microbiome have indicated that alterations in the abundance, composition, and function of the gut microbiome are intricately associated with human genotyping. Meanwhile, the gut microbiome can influence the pediatric health through the gut–brain, gut–lung, and gut–immune axes, covering diseases from the nervous, respiratory, and immune systems. **Figure 2**

Host control of the gut microbiome. (A) Immune: The dynamic equilibrium between the gut microbiome and host immune system is crucial for maintaining health. In most cases, the host regulates the composition, colonization, and metabolic products of the gut microbiome directly or indirectly through immune responses. Moreover, knockout of immune-related genes induces significant changes in the structure and function of the gut microbiome. (B) Nutrient: The host regulates the microbial colonization by restricting specific nutrients in the gut. For example, oligosaccharides in human milk are indigestible by infants themselves but serve as nutrients for specific early-life gut microbes, particularly bifidobacterial species, thereby conferring a selective advantage to these microbes. (C) Barriers: The host can also restrict the microbial contact and colonization through various barriers, including the chemical barrier formed by oxygen concentration gradients and the gut mucosa. Meanwhile, the mucus layer functions not only as a physical barrier but also regulates microbial distribution through selective permeability and the supply of nutrients.
